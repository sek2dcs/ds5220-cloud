# Use Of Generative AI In This Course

Some students have asked how I use Generative AI in this course, and I thought it would be both instructive and honest to share that with you.

I will keep these points simple but feel free to ask more in class or during office hours:

## Ways that I use GenAI (in no particular order):

- I often consult different public AI tools (Claude, Gemini) for good sources and examples of data.
- I occasionally ask AI tools (Claude, Gemini) their opinion on the "better" tool X vs. Y in the data engineering ecosystem, to see the results and reasoning for a preference.
- I sometimes ask AI tools (Claude, Gemini) for a different way to explain something, as a way of bouncing my own definition or examples off of something different.
- I sometimes offer my own summary points for a topic, or technology, or service, and ask AI (Claude, Gemini) if I am missing anything important that should also be mentioned.
- I often use GenAI tools (Claude Code, Cursor) to generate YAML manifest files, Dockerfiles, docker-compose files, GitHub Actions, etc. These boilerplate documents are difficult to write from scratch, and GenAI tools do an exceptional job with them. However, every line or parameter these tools define is already well-known to me. 
- When writing both labs and data projects, I always come with a sketch or outline of the learning objectives, technology stack, and specific tasks I want students to complete. And then I use AI tooling (Cursor, Claude Code) to help put that into a document, to format it consistently, to fill in gaps of explanation, and to organize the tasks into discrete logical steps. Sometimes I generate code templates or outlines on my own, and sometimes will have AI tools create those for me.
- Conversely, I have taken earlier data projects and asked GenAI tools (Claude Code) to determine the learning objectives for me, as a new way to identify the takeaways of a project.

## Ways that I DO NOT use GenAI:

- I do not use AI tools for any grading. That is to say, I will not pass student work into a tool and ask for a grade. I have never done this and don't plan on ever doing that.
- However, I sometimes use an AI tool (Claude Code) to write test suites (in Python, or in a linter) that I can run student code against, for code-level debugging output. That gives me more feedback I can consider when grading the work, alongside other factors.
- I do not use AI tools for writing emails or posts in Discord.
- I do not use AI tools to draft individualized feedback on your work, emails about your performance, or recommendations (for example, letters or references). What you read from me in those contexts is written by me.
- I do not use AI tools to make or justify decisions about academic integrity, extensions, exceptions to course policy, or similar matters. Those judgments stay with me and with the policies of the school and university.
